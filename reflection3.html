Personal Reflection 3: Social Extended Reality (XR) for Local Community

Project Description and Goal

Project 3 challenged our group to design a social extended reality (XR) solution to support a local community, leveraging XR platforms to address their specific needs. We chose art museum visitors (tourists and local residents) as our community, focusing on their desire for more interactive and engaging experiences when exploring artworks. Our goal was to empathize with museum visitors’ needs, ideate an XR-based solution to enhance their cultural exploration, and prototype an interactive mobile application called MuseAR with features like virtual artist avatars, time-lapse views, and interactive games . We conducted qualitative user evaluations, presented our work in class, and submitted a video demo. My contributions included leading the empathy phase, facilitating ideation and brainstorming, and handling the presentation and usability testing.

Learning and Execution Process

1. Empathizing with Users

I spearheaded the empathy phase to understand the needs of art museum visitors. I designed and distributed a Google Form questionnaire to 19 museum visitors at a local art museum, asking about their experiences with existing museum guides and their preferences for learning about artworks. Key findings revealed that visitors found current materials (e.g., PDF guides) text-heavy, hard to access, and lacking engaging context, with a preference for hidden stories and artist perspectives.

To complement the survey, I conducted a site visit to the Hong Kong Museum of Art, shadowing visitors and observing their behaviors. I noted that some googled artwork backgrounds on their smartphones, discussed artists’ life stories, and took photos, indicating a desire for more interactive and contextual information. I documented these observations with notes and photos in a Google Doc, which I presented to my group to justify focusing on museum visitors. The unmet need for interactive guidance and storytelling solidified our choice, as visitors were comfortable using smartphones, making AR a suitable solution.

2. Ideation and Brainstorming

I led the ideation phase, facilitating a brainstorming session to develop an XR solution for museum visitors. Using a LucidSpark board, we mapped our user pain points (e.g., unengaging text descriptions, insufficient context) and potential XR features. Inspired by slide 12’s point of view (POV), I proposed a POV: “Museum visitors need immersive, accessible XR tools to deepen their cultural exploration and enhance their understanding of artworks.” I suggested features like a “Time-Lapse” view to show artwork evolution and an interactive game to spot details, adapting ideas from the survey findings about storytelling preferences.

We analyzed existing solutions (PDF guides, audio guides, online searches) and their pros and cons, concluding that XR offered superior interactivity and immersion. My groupmate sketched a storyboard of a visitor, Maya, using an XR app to explore an artwork’s hidden story via a virtual artist avatar, which we speed-dated with peers. Feedback praised the immersive concept but suggested clearer instructions for XR interactions, shaping our final proposal for MuseAR. At last, we prioritized features like “Masterpiece Mystery” and “Art Aging” to meet visitors’ needs for engagement and storytelling.

3. Prototyping

While I didn’t lead the prototyping, my groupmates developed the prototype using Figma and Unity, implementing four main features: “Masterpiece Mystery” (virtual avatar and game) , “Art Around” (360-degree rotation), “Viewer Voice” (comment board), and “Art Aging” (time-lapse view). My empathy work ensured the prototype prioritized storytelling and interactivity, with QR codes for easy access.

4. Presentation and Usability Testing

I was responsible for the in-class presentation and usability testing. For the presentation, I organized our slides to cover our process: empathy findings, POV, ideation, prototype features, and user feedback. My groupmate narrated the video demo, showcasing a user accessing MuseAR via a QR code to explore a painting’s time-lapse and leave a comment on the “Viewer Voice” board. I emphasized how MuseAR addressed user needs for immersive storytelling, citing survey data (e.g., visitors wanting hidden stories) and site visit observations.

For usability testing, I conducted qualitative evaluations with some HKUST students I asked: “How helpful was the virtual artist compared to PDF guides?” and “Did the time-lapse view add value to your experience?” Feedback praised “Masterpiece Mystery” for being engaging and less wordy, though users noted unclear instructions for “Art Around” zoom controls. The “Viewer Voice” board was appreciated for interactivity but raised concerns about comment clutter. Lastly, we summarize the insights in our presentation and sharing them with my group to propose improvements like clearer UI instructions and comment moderation.

Personal Reflection

This project allow me to know the challenges museum visitors face with unengaging and inaccessible materials, which I hadn’t fully appreciated before. Leading the empathy phase was rewarding; designing the questionnaire and conducting site visits taught me to translate real-world observations into actionable design insights. The site visit, in particular, visitors google artwork details highlighted their hunger for deeper context, which XR could uniquely satisfy.

Facilitating ideation stretched my creative and leadership skills. Guiding the group to align on MuseAR’s features while incorporating peer feedback from speed dating was challenging but taught me the value of iterative refinement. Presenting and conducting usability testing were highlights; hearing users describe the time-lapse as “fun” and “must-have” validated our approach, though addressing headset discomfort and UI clarity showed me the importance of practical design considerations.

I used AI tools minimally, leveraging ChatGPT to draft initial questionnaire questions, which I refined based on museum context. Compared to Project 2, I leaned more into qualitative research and group facilitation, which boosted my confidence in user-centered design. Next time, I’d dive into prototyping tools like Figma to contribute technically and ensure tighter integration between empathy and implementation. This project inspired me to keep exploring XR’s potential to make cultural experiences more immersive and inclusive.
