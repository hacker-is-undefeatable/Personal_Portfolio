Personal Reflection 1: Designing an Animal-Friendly HKUST for Sloths

Project Description and Goal

This project tasked us with imagining HKUST as a campus welcoming visiting students from the animal kingdom, specifically focusing on sloths as an extreme case study. The goal was to identify potential user characteristics, needs and obstacles these animal students might face on campus, and propose solutions to make HKUST more accessible and friendly for them. Through ideation and brainstorming, we aimed to empathize with these unique newcomers, document challenges via a mindmap and present our ideas during our group presentation.

Learning and Execution Process

1. Researching

  • I began by researching sloth behaviors, abilities and habits, such as their slow movement, poor eyesight, preference for hanging and resting in trees and need for quiet, warm environments. I interviewed peers at HKUST to understand common challenges faced by newcomers (e.g., navigation issues, accessibility barriers and classroom accommodations) and extrapolated these to sloths.
  • I created sketches and notes during interviews, which I later organized into a Word file. I also go around campus areas (like staircases, lecture halls and classrooms) to visualize potential sloth challenges, such as inaccessible stairs or fast-paced lecture environments.

2. Ideation and Brainstorming

  • During the in-class session, I collaborated with my group to combine sloth characteristics with HKUST’s existing infrastructure. We identified four key areas: Campus Navigation, Lecture & Learning Experience, Food & Environment, and Social Integration. Using a whiteboard and sticky notes, we mapped out potential problems (e.g., sloths struggling with stairs, needing tree-like resting spaces) and corresponding needs (e.g., tactile navigation aids, AI-assisted note-taking).
  • I contributed to creating the mindmap, using a tool called Miro to visually organize our ideas. The mindmap served as a structured representation of our brainstorming, and also branch into categories and sub-issues/solutions.

3. Group Presentation

For the presentation, my groupmate helped prepare a quick overview of our mindmap, focusing on a specific user scenario: a sloth student navigating HKUST’s campus. Also, we show a GIF of a sloth moving slowly to illustrate the challenges and proposed solutions like “slow lanes” and “hanging-friendly classrooms.

Personal Reflection

This project deepened my understanding of empathy-driven design, particularly for non-human users. I learned about sloth biology and behavior, as well as how to apply universal design principles to extreme cases. I also improved my skills in mindmapping, presentation design and collaborative brainstorming, recognizing the value of structured documentation (e.g., mindmaps, photos, videos) in problem-solving.

I realized the importance of balancing creativity with practicality—while sloth accommodations like “artificial hanging zones” are imaginative, they must align with campus feasibility. I also learned to manage time effectively during interviews and ideation, ensuring we captured diverse perspectives without overwhelming our process.

This experience boosted my confidence in leading group projects and sharpened my critical thinking for unconventional problems, particularly when addressing unconventional and complex problems. I’m excited to apply critical thinking to later projects, enabling me to tackle different problems in varied contexts with deeper insight and innovative problem-solving.
